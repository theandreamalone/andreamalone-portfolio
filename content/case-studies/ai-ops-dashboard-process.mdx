I used stakeholder interviews to determine which information each persona needed first and what level of detail supported their decisions. Senior leadership needed concise situation briefs, impact summaries, escalation signals, and resource implications, while engineers and field operators needed access to root-cause detail, performance evidence, and operational next steps. I prioritized network status, active incidents, trending AI chat topics, and AI inquiries at the first layer, then used progressive disclosure and links into deeper workflows to balance glanceability with completeness. Rather than creating one overloaded dashboard, I treated role adaptation as the organizing principle and established a framework that could extend beyond the two primary personas to two additional roles.

## When executives disagreed about AI itself, not just its content

**A deeper disagreement surfaced beyond information needs: some executives
wanted AI-supported synthesis, while others preferred a traditional
dashboard with no AI present at all**, concerned that an AI layer would be
less trustworthy than going directly to the source data. This wasn't a
disagreement I resolved by winning anyone over — it stayed genuinely split.
Instead of treating it as a debate to argue through, I proposed treating it
as two legitimate executive personas rather than one audience with a wrong
opinion in it. The result was an AI on/off toggle: each executive could use
the dashboard in the mode they actually trusted, without the design forcing
a single answer onto a divided group.
