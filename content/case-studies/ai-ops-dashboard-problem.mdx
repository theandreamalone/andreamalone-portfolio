Senior leaders and engineers needed to understand active network incidents,
emerging themes, operational impact, and what required attention — during
live incidents, not after them.

A dashboard alone could show known metrics, statuses, and counts, but it
could not easily answer the questions that changed from incident to
incident: What is happening across the network right now? What is driving
this incident? Which issues are related? What changed since the last
update? What deserves escalation?

**AI belonged here because the problem was synthesis, not display.** The
work required interpreting changing conditions across incidents and
responding to open-ended questions whose answers depended on the incident,
the role, and the moment. A static module or filter cannot do that work
well.

The boundary mattered as much as the capability. Stable, repeatedly
monitored information stayed in modules, KPIs, incident briefings, and
trending topics — the dashboard's job. AI was assigned to investigation,
synthesis, and follow-up questions: the work static components could not
perform. I separated what users needed to monitor from what they needed to
investigate, and gave AI only the second job.
