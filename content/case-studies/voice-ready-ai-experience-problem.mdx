The AI response engine itself was already built to serve fundamentally different
audiences with different needs — executives who need business impact first, and
engineers or operations staff who need technical evidence first. That same divide
shows up in how people need to *interact* with the assistant, not just how they
read its output: someone working a live incident doesn't have the same bandwidth
to type as someone reviewing a summary afterward.

The question wasn't whether to add voice — it was where voice actually helps
versus where it gets in the way, and how to let both modes coexist without
forcing a user into one or the other.

## Why AI, not rules

Responders needed situation briefs, impact summaries, root-cause analysis,
current status, recommended actions, risks, and supporting evidence. Rules
could identify conditions — a threshold breach, a missing status, a known
incident pattern — but they could not reliably reconcile supporting and
conflicting signals, explain why a conclusion was reached, identify missing
information, compare alternative actions, adapt a response to the current
incident context, or communicate uncertainty in a useful way.

**I used AI where the product needed to interpret incomplete and sometimes
conflicting operational evidence, not simply detect a condition.** Rules
could trigger an alert; AI could explain the situation, surface missing
information, compare options, and show where human judgment was still
required. The task was interpretation, not classification — and that is the
line that justified AI in this product.
