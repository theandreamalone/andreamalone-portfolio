## From summary to evidence

Each role saw a small, AI-supported summary first — a situation brief for leadership, deeper operational detail for engineers — with a single click or tap into the full incident brief behind it. The classification step that decided what was surfacing (an active incident, a trend, a routine check-in) determined which summary type and evidence - displayed by an expansion component with further explanation- set populated the view. 

## Letting users configure their own view

A role-based default is still a default. **I proposed letting users choose
which data categories drive their content and insights**, and toggle or
reorder dashboard modules by drag-and-drop, rather than shipping one fixed
layout per role. Leadership and operational users don't just need different
information — they need different amounts of it visible at once, and that
shifts over time as priorities change. Making the settings screen a real
configuration surface, rather than a one-time onboarding step, let people
revisit and adjust their own view whenever they needed to.

## Reusing the response-trust pattern already established

Rather than designing a second, unrelated chat pattern for the dashboard's
embedded assistant, **I proposed carrying over the same response-trust
pattern** already established for Enterprise Network Operations Platform's standalone AI assistant:
affordances that let a user see how an answer was derived, instead of a flat,
black-box reply, plus follow-up prompts that keep exploration inside the
thread. This reflects the platform's shared AI reasoning engine — one
underlying response architecture, surfaced consistently wherever AI shows up.
It also meant the dashboard's chat inherited a pattern users had already
learned elsewhere, rather than asking them to relearn a second AI interaction
model.

## Alerts versus quiet updates

Not every update interrupted the user. Alerting was handled through a separate, manually configured notification layer rather than an AI decision — only priority-flagged items surfaced as an alert, while lower-priority changes updated quietly in the background. Keeping that distinction rule-based, rather than AI-triggered, kept escalation predictable for users under incident pressure.

## Voice-first parity, in progress

Voice-first interaction states were already approved and shipped in the chat experience. Those same patterns are being extended to desktop chat, with the update scheduled to ship shortly after this writing — not yet live at time of this case study.
